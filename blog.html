LLM Notes • Updated March 2026

Some Notes on LLMs

LLMs have already changed research, engineering, and knowledge work. I choose to embrace them, but the real edge now comes from judgment: data inspection, evaluation, post-training, system design, and knowing where the models fail.

POST-TRAINING AGENTS EVALUATION

My basic view is simple: LLMs are powerful tools with unusual strengths, and the right response is neither hype nor denial. Once everyone has access to strong models, the bottleneck moves upward: better questions, better evaluation, better systems, and better taste.

What I have learned from post-training work is that inspecting data is often more important than blindly training. The real bias, useful signal, hidden assumptions, and failure modes are buried in the data itself. If you do not look carefully, you are optimizing blind.

I also increasingly believe boring work should be delegated to agents. Save your own attention for the places where judgment, debugging, and prioritization actually matter. That is where human leverage still compounds the fastest.

The field moves too quickly for dogma. The best way to form opinions is still to build directly with the models and let contact with reality correct your abstractions.

Current Beliefs

  • Memorization matters less; evaluation and framing matter more.
  • Post-training and integration often matter as much as the base model.
  • The scarcest resource is taste and attention, not raw information.
  • The best understanding still comes from direct use, not distant argument.
Algorithms & Hiring

My Mixed Feelings About Coding Interviews

Coding interviews are still an imperfect but practical filter. They are not the same thing as research coding or production engineering, but the market uses them because they are standardized and efficient.

The useful response is not to keep complaining. It is to separate “interview coding” from “real coding,” accept the game for what it is, and rebuild algorithmic foundations with enough repetition to make the patterns second nature.

My own strongest lessons were: practice by category, review aggressively, explain every design step out loud, and optimize for thought process rather than memorized answers. In the LLM era, that matters even more.

What I Would Still Tell Candidates

  • Group problems by pattern instead of solving randomly.
  • Take notes and review or you will forget faster than you think.
  • Readable concise code beats messy brute force.
  • Let the interviewer see your reasoning, not just the final answer.

My coding interview repo still contains the raw notebooks and category notes that grew out of this process.
